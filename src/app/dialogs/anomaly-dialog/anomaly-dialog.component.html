Mean anomaly is the angle of a hypothetical body moving around the orbit at a constant angular velocity — it advances in exact proportion to elapsed time. True anomaly is the actual angle of the real body along its elliptical orbit, measured from periapsis.

@if (hasEccentricity) {

The two are identical at periapsis and apoapsis, and identical throughout a perfectly circular orbit (eccentricity 0) — the body moves at constant speed all the way round. As eccentricity increases the real body speeds up near periapsis and slows down near apoapsis, so true anomaly races ahead of mean anomaly in the first half of the orbit and falls behind in the second half, before the two meet again at periapsis.

} @else {

}

Both values are recalculated here from the recorded mean anomaly (M₀) and its timestamp: elapsed time since that timestamp, divided by the orbital period, gives the number of orbits completed, which advances M₀ by that many full turns. The Orbit list shows this propagated to the most recent observation timestamp anywhere in the system, so every body reads as one consistent snapshot; the values above also show it propagated to right now.
