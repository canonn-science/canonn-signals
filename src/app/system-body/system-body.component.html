Neutron star jet angle ‚Äî explanation

These jet cone half angles were measured from a small sample of neutron stars. The measurements were fitted to a smooth S-shaped curve (a sigmoid) and then adjusted with small quadratic corrections to improve accuracy for extreme stars.

Model constants

  • Amin = -83.8389
  • Amax = -60.8896
  • k = 2.2037
  • x0 = -5.0497
  • alpha = 0.001517
  • gamma_sr = 0.724671
  • gamma_rot = -0.025587
  • gamma_age = 0.045594

How the prediction is built

1) Combine the inputs into a single predictor:

x = ln(solarRadius / sqrt(rotationalPeriod)) + 0.001517 √ó ln(age)

2) Pass the predictor through the sigmoid (S-shaped) curve:

Angle_sigmoid = Amin + (Amax - Amin) / (1 + exp(-k * (x - x0)))

3) Add small quadratic corrections to account for outliers:

corrections = gamma_sr * ln(solarRadius)^2 + gamma_rot * ln(rotationalPeriod)^2 + gamma_age * ln(age)^2

4) Final prediction: Angle_pred = Angle_sigmoid + corrections

Note: the displayed model is a simplified regression of a small sample. The uncertainty is large ‚Äî the tooltip shows a conservative ¬±5¬∞ estimate.

The calculation uses the stored mean anomaly (M0) and its timestamp (t0). Steps:

  1. Compute elapsed time since the timestamp: Œît = now - t0 (in days).
  2. Compute the number of orbital cycles since then: cycles = Œît / P, where P is the orbital period (days).
  3. Advance the mean anomaly by cycles √ó 360¬∞ to get the current mean anomaly M:
  4. M = (M0 + cycles √ó 360¬∞) mod 360¬∞
  5. Find the angular distance to the event:

  6. Convert to time: Œît_event = (ŒîŒ∏ / 360¬∞) √ó P. The event date is now + Œît_event.

How is this determined?
Sidereal day: The sidereal day is the time it takes for a body to rotate once relative to the distant stars. This is equal to the rotational period shown above.
Solar day: The solar day is the time between successive sunrises for an observer on the surface. It is calculated as:
solarDay = 1 / |1/rotationalPeriod - 1/orbitalPeriod|
If the rotational and orbital periods are exactly equal (1:1 resonance), the solar day is infinite‚Äîthe sun never moves in the sky, and the same face always points at the parent body.
If the resonance is not 1:1 or the solar day is short, the body is not tidally locked, even if the game says so.
Axial tilt: Even on a tidally locked world, if the planet has a significant axial tilt, some regions may experience a day/night cycle as the star appears to move north and south in the sky over the course of an orbit. This can create areas with periodic sunlight and darkness despite tidal locking.
