Neutron star jet angle — explanation

These jet cone half angles were measured from a small sample of neutron stars. The measurements were fitted to a smooth S-shaped curve (a sigmoid) and then adjusted with small quadratic corrections to improve accuracy for extreme stars.

Model constants

  • Amin = -83.8389
  • Amax = -60.8896
  • k = 2.2037
  • x0 = -5.0497
  • alpha = 0.001517
  • gamma_sr = 0.724671
  • gamma_rot = -0.025587
  • gamma_age = 0.045594

How the prediction is built

1) Combine the inputs into a single predictor:

x = ln(solarRadius / sqrt(rotationalPeriod)) + 0.001517 × ln(age)

2) Pass the predictor through the sigmoid (S-shaped) curve:

Angle_sigmoid = Amin + (Amax - Amin) / (1 + exp(-k * (x - x0)))

3) Add small quadratic corrections to account for outliers:

corrections = gamma_sr * ln(solarRadius)^2 + gamma_rot * ln(rotationalPeriod)^2 + gamma_age * ln(age)^2

4) Final prediction: Angle_pred = Angle_sigmoid + corrections

Note: the displayed model is a simplified regression of a small sample. The uncertainty is large — the tooltip shows a conservative ±5° estimate.

What is {{ apoPeriDialogData.type === 'apo' ? 'apoapsis' : 'periapsis' }}?

Apoapsis is the point in the orbit farthest from the parent body; periapsis is the closest point. Their distances are computed from the orbital semi-major axis a and eccentricity e using:

Periapsis: a × (1 - e)
Apoapsis: a × (1 + e)

How is the next date estimated?

The calculation uses the stored mean anomaly (M0) and its timestamp (t0). Steps:

  1. Compute elapsed time since the timestamp: Δt = now - t0 (in days).
  2. Compute the number of orbital cycles since then: cycles = Δt / P, where P is the orbital period (days).
  3. Advance the mean anomaly by cycles × 360° to get the current mean anomaly M:
  4. M = (M0 + cycles × 360°) mod 360°
  5. Find the angular distance to the event:
    * For periapsis: Δθ = (360° − M) mod 360°
    * For apoapsis: Δθ = (180° − M) mod 360°
  6. Convert to time: Δt_event = (Δθ / 360°) × P. The event date is now + Δt_event.

A next-event estimate requires a recorded mean anomaly and timestamp. If that data is unavailable the application cannot compute the next occurrence.

What is tidal locking?
The tidal lock indicator is often misinterpreted. It does not always mean that the body faces the same direction relative to its parent body like the Moon faces the Earth. That is just a special case.

Tidal locking is the process by which tidal forces alter the rotation of a body until it reaches a long-term rotational equilibrium with its orbit. A body is considered tidally locked when its average rotational evolution has stabilised and the net tidal torque over an orbit is effectively zero (dω/dt ≈ 0).

ω (omega) means rotational speed — how fast the body spins.
dω/dt means "how the spin rate changes over time."

This equilibrium may occur in several forms depending on orbital eccentricity and axial tilt.

Special cases include:

  • Synchronous rotation (1:1 resonance)
    The rotation period equals the orbital period and the same hemisphere always faces the parent body. Example: Earth's Moon. However, axial tilt can result in some surface seasonality over the course of an orbit.
  • Higher-order spin–orbit resonances
    The body rotates in a stable ratio other than 1:1. Example: Mercury's 3:2 resonance, where it rotates three times every two orbits.
