What does the tidal-lock flag mean?
The flag is set by the game when a body's rotation has settled into equilibrium with its orbit — its rotational period matches its orbital period. It is often misread as "one face is permanently lit" or "the same side always points at the parent." That fully-frozen, one-hemisphere-always-facing picture (like the Moon toward Earth) is only the idealised case: zero axial tilt and a perfectly circular orbit. The flag does not guarantee those conditions hold.

More precisely, tidal locking is the process by which tidal forces alter the rotation of a body until it reaches a long-term rotational equilibrium with its orbit. A body is considered tidally locked when its average rotational evolution has stabilised and the net tidal torque over an orbit is effectively zero (dω/dt ≈ 0).

ω (omega) means rotational speed — how fast the body spins.
dω/dt means "how the spin rate changes over time."

Because the lock balances the average torque, the body can still rock back and forth slightly relative to its parent over each orbit. This rocking — called optical libration — is driven by orbital eccentricity and axial tilt, and it is what adds seasonality and variation in illumination even on a "locked" world.

Special cases include:

  • Synchronous rotation (1:1 resonance)
    The rotation period equals the orbital period and, on average, the same hemisphere faces the parent body. Example: Earth's Moon.
    It is not perfectly frozen, though: axial tilt makes the sub-stellar point drift north–south, and orbital eccentricity makes it drift east–west (the body orbits faster near periapsis than it spins, then slower near apoapsis). Together these add seasonality and a band near the terminator that swings between day and night over each orbit.
  • Higher-order spin–orbit resonances
    The body rotates in a stable ratio other than 1:1. Example: Mercury's 3:2 resonance, where it rotates three times every two orbits.
